Let's start with the word "etymology".

Etymology, from etymos (Greek), true, real + logos (Greek), word, meaning. Etymology traces a word back to its origininal meaning.

## logos

When logos is taken into English, it's usually of the form -logy. One of the key meaning of -logy is the study of. For example:

 - geology: from geo (Greek), earth + logos. geology means the study of the earth.
   - geography: description of the earth's surface. from geo + graphein, to write
   - George: originally a farmer, from geo + ergon, work
   - geometry: measurement of the earth, from geo + metron, measurement
 - biology: the study of life, from bios (Greek), life + logos.
 - dermatology: the study of the skin, from derma (Greek): skin + logos
   - hypodermic: under the skin, from hypos (Greek), under + derma
   - dermatitis: inflamation of the skin, from derma + -itis, inflamation
 - zoology: the study of animals, from zoion (Greek), animal + logos
 - theology: the study of religion. Originally means the study of god, from theo (Greek), god + logos
   - atheist: a person who doesn't believe in God, from a-, not + theos
 - psychology: the study of the mind, from psyche (Greek), spirit, soul, or mind + logos
 - pathology: the study of diseases, from pathos (Greek), suffering or disease + logos
 - archaeology: the study of ancient things, from Arkhaios (Greek), ancient + logos
 - sociology: the study of societies, from socius (Latin), companion + logos
 - anthropology: the study of humans, from anthropos (Greek), mankind + logos
   - misanthrope: a person who hates other people, from misein (Greek), to hate + anthropos
   - philanthropist: a person who gives money and time to help other people, from philein (Greek), to love + anthropos
   - philosophy: the study of ideas about knowledge, truth, the nature and meaning of life, etc., "love of wisdom", from philein + sophos (Greek), wisdom
 - astrology: the study of stars, from astron (Greek), star + logos. Astrology studies the influence of planets and stars on human events. The actual science of the stars is astronomy. 
   - astronomy: the scientific study of stars, from astron + nomos, arrangement, law, order
   - disaster: from dis-, against + astron
   - astronaut: "a sailor in the stars", from astron + nautes (Greek), sailor
   - cosmonaut: a Russian astronaut, "a sailor in the universe", from kosmos (Greek), universe + nautes
 - cardiology: the study of the heart, from kardia (Greek), heart + logos
   - cardiograph: the machine that "writes about heart", from kardia + graphein, to write
   - cardiogram: the curve made by cardiograph
 - ophthalmology: the study of the eyes, from ophthalmos (Greek), eye + logos
   - monocle: "one eye", from mono-, one + oculus (Latin), eye
   - binocular: "two eyes", from bi-, two + oculus
   - optometry: the measurement of eyes, from optikos (Greek), eye or vision + metron, measurement
   - optician: one who makes glasses
   - oculist: an ophthalmologist or optometrist, from oculus + -ist

-logy could also mean word or speech:

 - tautology: a statement that repeates an idea in a way that is not useful, "the same word", from tauto (Greek), same + logos
 - eulogy: a speech that praises someone who has died, "a good speech", from eu- (Greek), good + logos
   - euphemism: a mild or pleasant word or phrase that is used instead of one that is unpleasant or offensive, from eu- + pheme, voice
   - euthanasia: "good death", from eu- + thanatos (Greek), death
   - euphony: pleasing sound, from eu- + phone (Greek), sound
     - cacophony: unpleasant sound, from kakos (Greek), bad, harsh, or ugly + phone
     - telephone: "sound from far", from tele (Greek), far + phone
     - television: from tele + vision

## Good or bad?

Bonus is the Latin adjective for good, and bene is its adverb. On the opposite side, malus is Latin for bad, evil.

 - benign, benignant: harmless, from bene
    - malign, malignant: causing harm, evil, from malus
   - malign could also be a verb, meaning to say bad things about sb. publicly
 - benediction: prayer, from bene + dico, dictus (Latin), to say or tell
   - malediction: curse, from malus + dico, dictus
   - valedictorian: the highest ranking student of a graduating class who delivers a speech at the graduation ceremony, from vale (Latin), goodbye + dictus
 - beneficent: doing or making good, from bene + facere (Latin), to do or make
   - maleficent: doing or making bad, from malus + facere (Latin), to do or make
 - benefactor: philanthropist, from bene + facere
   - malefactor: criminal, from malus + facere
 - benevolent: kind, "wishing good", from bene + volo (Latin), to wish
   - malevolent: not kind, malicious, "wishing bad", from malus + volo
 - beneficiary: one who is helped by sth., from bene + facere
 - malice: bad, evil intent, from malus
   - malicious: adj. of malice
 - malnutrition: from malus + nutrition
